## The situation

My school didn't offer AP Physics. I found out in February that the exam was in May and that I could register as an external candidate. I had about four weeks before the exam — minus the time I needed to keep up with my regular schoolwork.

So I decided to try.

## What I used

My main resource was Khan Academy for video explanations and the College Board's practice exams. I also made my own handwritten notes in GoodNotes — not because I think note-taking is the most efficient way to learn, but because writing things out forces me to slow down and actually think.

For AP Physics II specifically, I used Flipping Physics on YouTube. The explanations for electromagnetic induction are the clearest I've found anywhere.

## The strategy that worked

I quickly realized that reading or watching videos was a trap. I could sit for two hours, feel like I understood everything, then fail a practice problem on the exact same concept.

The strategy that worked was this: watch a short video to get the gist of a topic, then immediately try problems. Don't wait until you feel "ready." You won't feel ready. Just try the problem and use the video as a reference when you're stuck.

Some specific things that helped:

**Free body diagrams for everything.** Every mechanics problem, no matter how simple, gets a diagram. It forces you to identify all the forces before you start writing equations.

**Dimensional analysis as a sanity check.** If my answer has units of meters per second squared and I'm calculating a distance, something is wrong. This caught probably a third of my errors.

**Re-doing wrong problems the next day.** I would mark every problem I got wrong, then redo those problems — without looking at the solution — 24 hours later. If I couldn't do it, I went back to the concept.

## What didn't click until late

Electromagnetic induction (Faraday's law, Lenz's law) didn't make sense to me for two weeks. The formulas were easy to memorize, but I didn't understand *why* a changing magnetic flux induces an EMF.

What finally helped was thinking about it from the perspective of the free charges in the conductor. If the conductor moves through a magnetic field, the charges experience a force (F = qv × B). That force pushes charges in one direction, creating a current. Faraday's law is just a general statement of that effect. Once I had that picture in my head, the problems became much easier.

## The result

I got a 5 on both exams. I don't think I could have done that without the self-imposed deadline. There's something about knowing that the exam is in four weeks that sharpens your focus in a way that a semester-long course doesn't.
